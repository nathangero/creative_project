Recitation
A form of plainchant. The notes don't vary much. Almost seems static. There's a repeated note that is sung.
Drone
A pitch being sustained in singing.
Types: A portable organ. A wheel that would grind up against a string. A person in a choir that would hold a pitch.
Harmony
A musical material that enhances or supports the melody. Basically what Polynesians are really good at.
Meter
A pattern regularly accented beats and unaccented beats. Deals with the time signature.

-Duple: a recurring pattern of one accented beat followed by one unaccented beat. Most marches
-Triple: A recurring pattern of one accented beat followed by two unaccented beats. Waltzes and other dances.
-Quadruple: pattern of one accented beat followed by a light beat, then a somewhat heavy beat, and then a final light beat. Prevalent in music like pop.
-Non: there's just no pattern. Gregorian chant
-Mixed: Changes between one pattern of meter to another. "The constant shifting just makes it more difficult to tap your toes as you listen."
-Irregular: the meter isn't easily divisible by 2 or 3. So like having 5 or 7 beats per measure.

Troubadour songs
Created by members of the nobility and educated musicians who worked in the homes of the wealthy as their personal, in-house composers.
Secular music: drinking songs, glorious battles or knights saving princesses songs, and love and/or lust songs.
You still hear these kinds of songs at formal concerts, Medieval (and Renaissance) fairs, Shakespearean festivals, and similar events around the world.

-Alba: Song about two lovers parting as the sun rises, often warned by a friend standing guard of the approach of one or both of their spouses
-Escondig: Song in which a man apologizes to a lady for his inappropriate behavior.
